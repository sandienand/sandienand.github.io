My GRC Career Sprint, Take 2. The Adaptive Plan for Leadership

Published: Oct 5, 2025 by The Integrity Protocol

Hi, all. Following last week's update, I'm excited to share a deeper dive into the 'how' of my pivot into Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) leadership. This isn't a sudden career change; it's a strategic evolution. My years in financial services, mastering AML/KYC compliance, process optimisation, and large-scale team leadership, have given me a robust foundation in the 'G' and 'R' of GRC. Now, I'm focused on acquiring the rigorous technical understanding required for modern cyber-centric risk.

As someone with ADHD, I know a rigid plan is often a recipe for burnout. My approach is designed to be constructively adaptable, ensuring curiosity fuels my momentum without derailing my ultimate objective. This isn't a static six-month syllabus; it's an adaptive, three-phase sprint.

The Adaptive Three-Phase GRC Sprint

The core of this "Take 2" sprint is about stacking the *technical* skills (the 'C' for Controls and the *Cyber* in Risk) onto my existing *Governance and Compliance* expertise. Each phase builds on the last, ensuring theory is immediately tested with application.

Phase 1: Foundational Technical Mastery (The Core)

This phase is non-negotiable. I need to move from knowing *what* security controls are to knowing *how* they are implemented and measured in a modern context, particularly in the cloud.

  • Core Focus: Cloud Security Fundamentals (AWS/Azure security controls, IaaS/PaaS risk models), Network Architecture (understanding the concepts behind Zero Trust), and Cybersecurity Frameworks (NIST, ISO 27001).
  • Success Metric: A complete, practical understanding of the CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability) and how it informs cloud control design.

Phase 2: Application and Automation (The 'R' and 'C')

This stage leverages my background in process optimisation (Power Automate, VBA) and transforms it into GRC automation. Governance shouldn't be manual paper-shuffling; it should be automated, auditable, and repeatable.

  • Core Focus: GRC Platforms (e.g., ServiceNow GRC, AuditBoard), Audit Logging and Monitoring to detect control failure, and the principles of Compliance as Code (CaC).
  • Success Metric: Translating control failure data into actionable risk insights for senior leadership using data analysis tools (PivotTables, VLOOKUPs) to find true risk signal in the noise.

Phase 3: Leadership and Ethical Integration (The 'G' and The Protocol)

The ultimate goal is GRC leadership, which requires translating technical risk into business language and applying my Integrity Protocol Framework to emerging technological frontiers.

  • Core Focus: AI Governance (bias, transparency, explainability), Data Ethics, and Strategic Stakeholder Negotiation (managing the tension between security/risk and business enablement).
  • Success Metric: A refined theory of the Integrity Protocol that explicitly links ethical principles (Hold Only What You Must, Secure What You Hold) to technical mechanisms like Zero Trust.

Why the Adaptive Approach Matters

The biggest lesson from my previous attempt was that the world of GRC moves too fast for a static, linear syllabus. By using an adaptive model, I can:

  • Timebox Learning: Spend focused periods on one technical area, then immediately test that knowledge against a real-world case study (like a major Australian data breach).
  • Maintain Momentum: Structure the sprint to accommodate the necessary shift in focus when a high-impact topic (like a new piece of legislation) suddenly demands attention.
  • Ensure Accountability: Use this platform to document the process, turning my learning journey into a governance audit trail for myself.

The pivot is challenging, but the path to leadership is clearer than ever. I'm ready to move past the planning and start sharing the technical insights I'm gaining.
