Navigating My New Path: A Flexible Plan for GRC Leadership

Hi, all. Following the deep dive into the massive data breach (Post 3), I’m excited to share a deeper look into the 'how' of my pivot into Governance, Risk, and Compliance (GRC) leadership. This isn't a sudden career change; it's a strategic evolution.

My years in financial services, mastering AML/KYC compliance, process optimisation, and large-scale team leadership, have given me a robust foundation in the 'G' and 'R' of GRC. Now, I'm focused on acquiring the rigorous technical understanding required for modern cyber-centric risk, which is what the breach analysis proved is missing in too many organisations.

As someone with ADHD, I know a rigid plan is often a recipe for burnout. My approach is designed to be constructively adaptable, ensuring curiosity fuels my momentum without derailing my ultimate objective: GRC leadership in the age of AI. I need to manage my energy, not just my time.

The Three-Part Flexible Strategy

The path is broken down into three phases, designed to be executed flexibly. If I lose focus on one area, I can pivot to another to maintain learning momentum, then circle back.

1. Foundational Deep Dive: Speaking the Language of Tech

Goal: Gain technical literacy to credibly challenge engineers and interpret audit reports. This means understanding the core building blocks of modern systems, not becoming an expert coder.

  • Cyber Fundamentals: Core concepts of the CIA Triad, network security, and common vulnerabilities (e.g., OWASP).
  • Cloud Architecture: Basics of AWS/Azure, focusing on Identity and Access Management (IAM), data storage, and encryption.
  • Data Flow Mapping: Practical skills in visualising how data moves through a system—crucial for enforcing the Integrity Protocol’s Minimisation principle.

2. Skill Layering: The GRC Automation Toolkit

Goal: Acquire practical skills to build automated governance controls—moving GRC from paper-based audits to digital monitoring.

  • Python for GRC: Scripting basic tasks like risk metrics calculation, data sampling, and automating documentation checks.
  • SQL/NoSQL Basics: Ability to query and validate data residency, retention, and access logs directly from databases.
  • Audit Automation: Exploring tools that integrate into Continuous Integration/Continuous Deployment (CI/CD) pipelines to check for policy violations *before* code goes live.

3. Execution & Artifact Creation: Proving Competence

Goal: Produce portfolio-ready work* that demonstrates competence in both governance and technical application.

  • AI Governance Policy: Drafting a Model Risk Policy for a hypothetical Generative AI use case (e.g., content summarisation).
  • System Security Matrix: Creating a cross-reference between a major regulation (like the CDR or new AI laws) and the specific technical controls required to enforce it.
  • Simulated Audit: Performing a dry-run audit of a publicly available open-source project or application, focusing on the Minimisation and Accountability principles.

Managing Energy, Not Time

The critical differentiator here is the focus on Managing Energy, Not Time. A traditional study schedule demands adherence, which often fails under the pressure of ADHD hyper-focus or distraction.

This flexible structure allows me to lean into my strengths. If the SQL learning feels like a slog, I can switch to the hands-on fun of Python scripting or dive deep into a specific AI policy paper. The learning is layered, meaning every deep dive contributes to the final Execution Phase, regardless of the order.

It's about having multiple high-value paths available at all times. This approach ensures I maintain high output and enthusiasm, keeping my curiosity high and burnout low.
